# Frequency-dependent head-tissue conductivity and permittivity for EEG source localization and tES

Two facts sit uneasily together in this subfield. Head-modeling pipelines almost universally treat tissue as purely ohmic, assigning each compartment a single real conductivity and solving Laplace's equation. Yet the tissues themselves are strongly dispersive at the very frequencies EEG and tACS operate in (grey matter's relative permittivity runs to ~10⁷ around 10 Hz), and whether that dispersion matters has been argued both ways for two decades. The recent modeling literature has largely resolved the *magnitude* of the error from neglecting permittivity while leaving open whether it is ever *exploitable*. This review extracts the quantitative findings across four threads and closes with a simulation testing the "colored-filters" idea directly.

## Quasi-static approximation and permittivity error

The cleanest recent statement of the problem is [Gaugain et al. 2023](https://doi.org/10.1088/1741-2552/acb14d), the anchor for thread (a) (the J. Neural Eng. paper you cited as "Gomez"; its first author is Gabriel Gaugain, with Marom Bikson as senior coauthor). Working from an anatomical head model, they separate two distinct approximations that the field often conflates. The **quasi-static approximation** proper (QSA) retains tissue as a *lossy dielectric* — complex permittivity, capacitive currents — but drops wave propagation and induction; the **static** formulation goes further and drops permittivity entirely, leaving a purely ohmic medium. These are not the same simplification, and they fail at opposite ends of the spectrum.

The QSA (permittivity retained) is excellent: the relative error of the electric field stays **below 1% up to 1.43 MHz**, and the full-wave-to-QS error grows *with* frequency, crossing 1% only in the MHz range. The static formulation (permittivity neglected) is the opposite: its error is **over 1% across the entire spectrum and as high as 20% in the brain at 10 Hz**, and it grows *toward low frequency*. In the 10–40 Hz band used for tACS, the average static-vs-QS error in brain tissue exceeds 20%, rising to ~50% for the 97.5th-percentile field locations in their 3-D model. At the neuron level the discrepancy reaches **22% in the radial ("sense") E-field at the focusing point**, enough to shift predicted pyramidal-cell firing times. For pulsed (non-harmonic) waveforms the capacitive term also prevents the waveform distortion that a purely ohmic model introduces — a 22.7% difference at the positive-phase maximum for a monophasic pulse, 42.9% for biphasic. Their conclusion is the one that matters for your calibration project: QSA is safe for tACS, but "neglecting permittivity introduces significant error," and "reliable low-frequency dielectric data are needed for accurate transcranial current stimulation modeling."

This distinction is formalized in the companion review [Wang, Bikson et al. 2024](https://doi.org/10.1088/1741-2552/ad625e), which decomposes QSA into four separable assumptions: (A1) no propagation/induction, (A2) linearity, (A3) purely resistive tissue, and (A4) non-dispersive tissue. The key point for thread (c) is that A3 and A4 are independent: a model can carry a *complex* conductivity with genuine frequency dependence through its capacitive component "regardless of assumption A4" — i.e. whether or not σ and ε are themselves dispersive. This is the hook for embedding permittivity into an otherwise-QSA pipeline without abandoning the Laplace-type solver, via parallel or iterative frequency-by-frequency solves. The historical thread runs back through [Bossetti, Birdno & Grill 2008](https://doi.org/10.1088/1741-2560/5/1/005), who first quantified that neglecting the capacitive component biases neural-stimulation potentials (3–16% depending on pulse duration for DBS), and [Plonsey & Heppner 1967](https://doi.org/10.1007/bf02476917), the original quasi-stationarity analysis. The ECT modeling community has adopted the same framing ([Unal et al. 2023](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.brs.2023.03.007)).

## Dispersion data: Gabriel 1996 and its limits

Thread (b) rests almost entirely on one body of work: the Gabriel trilogy ([literature survey](https://doi.org/10.1088/0031-9155/41/11/001); [measurements 10 Hz–20 GHz](https://doi.org/10.1088/0031-9155/41/11/002); [parametric Cole–Cole models](https://doi.org/10.1088/0031-9155/41/11/003), all 1996). Part III fits each tissue to a four-term Cole–Cole dispersion,

σ*(ω) = σᵢ + jωε₀[ε∞ + Σₖ Δεₖ / (1 + (jωτₖ)^(1−αₖ))],

whose four relaxations correspond to the classical α (Hz to kHz, counterion/membrane), β (kHz to MHz, Maxwell–Wagner interfacial), and γ (GHz, water) dispersions plus an intermediate. The parameters are the de-facto standard, embedded in the IT'IS/Hasgall tissue database that SimNIBS and most FEM pipelines draw from. Two properties matter for your band. First, the **α-dispersion dominates 1–100 Hz**: grey-matter ε_r falls from ~4.5×10⁷ at 1 Hz to ~4×10⁶ at 100 Hz while σ rises from ~0.02 to ~0.09 S/m, both changing by factors of several *within* the EEG band. Second, the low-frequency end of the Gabriel data is the weakest: measurements below ~100 Hz are sparse and electrode-polarization-prone, a limitation Gabriel's group themselves revisited ([Gabriel, Peyman & Grant 2009](https://doi.org/10.1088/0031-9155/54/16/002), "conductivity of tissue at frequencies below 1 MHz"). This is precisely the sub-kHz regime Gaugain et al. flag as under-measured, and it is the regime your DTI-free calibration would need to constrain.

For the individual compartments you model: CSF is nearly non-dispersive and near-resistive (σ≈1.5–2.0 S/m, low loss tangent) because its high ionic conductivity swamps the displacement current; **compact vs. spongy skull** differ several-fold in both σ and the trabecular water fraction that drives their β-dispersion, the single largest source of leadfield uncertainty; scalp and grey/white matter carry the strongest α-dispersion. The wide spread in *reported* values, independent of frequency, is catalogued by [McCann, Pisano & Beltrachini 2019](https://doi.org/10.1007/s10548-019-00710-2), whose review of human head conductivities documents skull-conductivity estimates spanning nearly an order of magnitude; that spread is arguably a larger error source than dispersion itself and is the direct motivation for calibration over table look-up.

## Complex admittivity and dispersive head models

Thread (c) — writing σ*(ω)=σ(ω)+jωε(ω) into the forward model — is conceptually settled and computationally cheap but rarely done in EEG practice. The admittivity is complex and frequency-dependent, so the forward operator becomes a complex, band-specific leadfield L(ω); solving Laplace's equation per frequency with complex coefficients is a lumped-RC (dispersive) generalization of the standard solve ([Wang, Bikson et al. 2024](https://doi.org/10.1088/1741-2552/ad625e)). Where the field genuinely disagrees is on whether this is *necessary for EEG* (as opposed to tES). The EEG-forward-uncertainty literature — e.g. [Vorwerk et al. 2016](https://doi.org/10.1002/hbm.23263) on conductivity uncertainty and model complexity — has generally treated permittivity as negligible for scalp potentials, on the argument that EEG sources are narrowband and low-frequency and that skull-conductivity uncertainty dwarfs any capacitive term. The tES modeling side (Gaugain; Bossetti–Grill) argues the opposite for the *induced* field. The two positions are partly reconcilable: the permittivity error is largest exactly where the ohmic assumption is applied to a *broadband* or *pulsed* dose, and smallest for a steady narrowband sinusoid whose single-frequency conductivity can simply be substituted.

The deeper physical disagreement concerns the *origin* and even the *sign* of tissue frequency-filtering. [Bédard & Destexhe 2011](https://doi.org/10.1103/physreve.84.041909) argue that Maxwell–Wagner interfacial effects and ionic diffusion make the extracellular medium significantly non-resistive and frequency-filtering, a view supported by some impedance-spectroscopy work. Against this, [Logothetis, Kayser & Oeltermann 2007](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuron.2007.07.027) measured cortical impedance in vivo in monkeys and found it essentially **frequency-independent and real** across the physiological band, and [Miceli et al. 2017](https://doi.org/10.1523/eneuro.0291-16.2016) similarly report a near-resistive cortical spectrum. A microscopic-mechanism account of how confined-electrolyte double layers produce the macroscopic σ*(ω) and Maxwell–Wagner behavior appears in [Solis & Jadhao 2023](https://doi.org/10.1088/1361-6560/accf5b). This is the live controversy to flag: **whether the extracellular medium is resistive or dispersive in the EEG band is not settled experimentally**, and it bears directly on whether your calibration should even carry an imaginary part.

Direct intracranial validation of the (real-conductivity) forward field exists — [Opitz et al. 2016](https://doi.org/10.1038/srep31236) and [Huang et al. 2017](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.brs.2016.11.066) measured tES-induced fields with implanted electrodes in humans — but neither resolves the permittivity question because both work at effectively a single low frequency. On the data side, [Marmin et al. 2025](https://doi.org/10.1016/j.neuroimage.2025.121559) extend water-content-based EPT to give age-specific σ(ω), ε(ω) for grey and white matter, but only above 50 MHz: useful for the model *form* and for pediatric work, not for the 1–100 Hz band.

## Frequency-resolved leadfields: an observability test

Thread (d) is the most speculative: could per-band leadfields L(ω), built from the complex admittivity, span *different* column spaces and thereby add observable rank — a "colored-filters" gain in source resolution from combining bands? I tested this with a three-shell concentric-sphere head (brain / compact skull / scalp; 64 electrodes, 200 cortical radial dipoles), computing the complex leadfield analytically per frequency from Cole–Cole σ*(ω), and measuring principal angles between the dominant column subspaces of L(ωᵢ) at realistic EEG SNR. The solver reproduces the analytic homogeneous-sphere leadfield to machine precision (max relative error 4.6×10⁻¹⁶).

The answer is negative, for a specific and instructive reason. Across 1–100 Hz the dominant-subspace principal angles are small: the maximum angle between colspace(L(f)) and colspace(L(10 Hz)) in the top-20 directions is **6.9° at 1 Hz and 6.1° at 100 Hz**, and the complex-vs-permittivity-free comparison at matched frequency peaks at only **4.2°** (near 10 Hz, where the loss tangent is largest). Stacking the real and imaginary parts of nine bands (1–100 Hz) into one tall system adds **zero** resolvable source dimensions relative to a single band at every SNR from 20 to 60 dB. The mechanism is that the only frequency-dependent quantity is the per-degree complex shell gain gₙ(ω); the angular kernel (the Legendre basis) is fixed, so changing frequency merely *reweights* the same spatial harmonics rather than rotating into new ones. The reweighting is real but modest (high spatial frequencies are relatively enhanced ~15–20% at 100 Hz versus 1 Hz), so the leadfields are **near-collinear, not orthogonal**. A multi-band measurement of the same source therefore behaves like repeated looks through slightly different-colored but overlapping filters: it improves SNR on the shared subspace, but does not open genuinely new observable directions the way adding electrodes or exploiting anisotropy can.

The practical implication for your program is that frequency-resolved leadfields are worth carrying for *forward-model accuracy* (the 20% permittivity error is real) and possibly for *conductivity calibration*, because gₙ(ω)'s frequency signature is a fingerprint of the σ*(ω) parameters and could help identify them from broadband active-stimulation data, but not as a route to inverse-resolution gain. That reframes thread (d) from "free super-resolution" to "an identifiability handle for calibration," which is squarely on your stated goal. The obvious next step is to repeat this in a realistic anisotropic FEM head (the near-collinearity may loosen slightly where white-matter tensors break the spherical harmonic degeneracy) and to run the forward direction: propagate the McCann-scale conductivity spread and the Gaugain 20% permittivity term through your MNE/eLORETA pipeline to see which dominates localization error on the LEMON data.

## Where the field stands

The magnitude question (thread a) is settled: neglecting permittivity via a purely ohmic model errs the induced field by ~20% in brain at tACS frequencies and distorts pulsed waveforms, while the proper QSA (permittivity retained) is accurate to <1% throughout the EEG/tACS range. The data question (thread b) is settled in *form* (the Gabriel Cole–Cole models) but weakest exactly in your 1–100 Hz band, where new low-frequency dielectric measurements are the field's stated need. The mechanism question (thread c) is genuinely open: in-vivo impedance spectroscopy (Logothetis; Miceli) sees a near-resistive cortex, while interfacial-polarization theory (Bédard–Destexhe) and the tES modeling community argue for meaningful dispersion. And the observability question (thread d), by the simulation here, resolves against a resolution gain but *for* using σ*(ω) as a calibration fingerprint.
